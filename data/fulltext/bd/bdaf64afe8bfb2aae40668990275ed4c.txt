Abstract
Background and aims: The gut microbiome exerts important roles in health, e.g., func-
tions in metabolism and immunology. These functions are often exerted via short-chain fatty
acid (SCFA) production by gut bacteria. Studies demonstrating causal relationships between in-
terventions targeting the microbiome and clinical outcomes are limited. This study aimed to
show a causal relationship between microbiome modulation through ’¼übre intervention and
health.
Methods and results: This randomized, double-blind, cross-over study included 65 healthy sub-
jects, aged 45e70 years, with increased metabolic risk (i.e., body mass index [BMI] 25e30 kg/
m2, low to moderate daily dietary ’¼übre intake, <30g/day). Subjects took daily a ’¼übre mixture
of Acacia gum and carrot powder or placebo for 12 weeks, with an 8-week wash-out period.
Faecal samples for measurement of SCFAs and microbiome analysis were collected every 4
weeks. Before and after each intervention period subjects underwent the mixed-meal PhenFlex
challenge Test (PFT). Health effects were expressed as resilience to the stressors of the PFT and as
fasting metabolic and in’¼éammatory state. The ’¼übre mixture exerted microbiome modulation,
with an increase in b-diversity (p < 0.001). a-diversity was lower during ’¼übre mixture intake
compared to placebo after 4, 8 and 12 weeks (p Z 0.002; p Z 0.012; p Z 0.031). There was
no effect observed on faecal SCFA concentrations, nor on any of the primary clinical outcomes
(In’¼éammatory resilience: p Z 0.605, Metabolic resilience: p Z 0.485).
Conclusion: Although the intervention exerted effects on gut microbiome composition, no effects
on SCFA production, on resilience or fasting metabolic and in’¼éammatory state were observed in
this cohort.

2.1. Study design and population
A double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled crossover
study was conducted with two 12-week periods separated
by 8 weeks wash-out period, in 65 healthy volunteers
(Figure S1). In order to maximize effect of the intervention,
participants with increased metabolic risk were selected, i.e.

’¼übre intake <30g. The study was conducted at the Centre
for Human Drug Research (CHDR), Leiden, The Netherlands,
between August 2020 and July 2021. All participants gave
written informed consent according to Declaration of Hel-
sinki recommendations prior to any study-related activity.
